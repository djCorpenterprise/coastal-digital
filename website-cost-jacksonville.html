How Much Does a Website Cost in Jacksonville FL in 2025?

The honest answer is: it depends. But that's not very useful if you're actually trying to budget. Here's a clear breakdown of what you'll pay at every price point — and more importantly, what you actually get.

If you've searched "website cost Jacksonville" and gotten anything from $500 to $50,000, you're not confused — that range is real. The price of a website varies wildly based on who builds it, how it's built, and what it needs to do. This guide breaks it all down so you can make an informed decision for your business.

The Four Price Tiers — What Each One Actually Gets You

Tier 1: DIY Website Builders ($0–$50/month)

Squarespace, Wix, and GoDaddy Website Builder fall into this category. You pay a monthly subscription, pick a template, and drag and drop your content in. For a brand new business that needs something live fast and has almost no budget, this is a reasonable starting point.

What you give up: design quality, performance, flexibility, and SEO. Template sites are slow to load, hard to customize beyond surface-level changes, and almost impossible to rank locally because every other business on the platform shares similar code structure. You also don't own anything — cancel the subscription and your site disappears.

Best for: Hobby businesses, side projects, or temporary landing pages. Not recommended for any business serious about growth.

Tier 2: Freelancer or Template-Based Custom ($1,500–$5,000)

This is where most small businesses in Jacksonville end up when they hire a freelancer off Fiverr, Upwork, or a local referral. The freelancer typically takes a WordPress theme, customizes it with your branding and content, and hands it off. You get a real website you own, usually with a decent design.

The tradeoffs: quality varies enormously depending on the freelancer. Some are excellent; many are not. You may end up with bloated code, a theme that slows your site down, or a site that looks great but isn't built with SEO or conversions in mind. Support after delivery is also inconsistent — some freelancers disappear after the final invoice.

Best for: Businesses with a tight budget who need a functional site quickly and understand the limitations.

Tier 3: Custom Boutique Agency ($2,000–$8,000)

This is where purpose-built websites live. Smaller agencies and experienced independents build your site from scratch with a lean, clean codebase rather than a bloated theme. The design is unique to your brand, the code is tight, and the whole thing is built with your target customer and Google rankings in mind from day one.

At this level, you should expect: a custom design that reflects your brand, a fast-loading site (under 2 seconds), mobile-first development, on-page SEO built in, and a team you can actually reach when something needs to change. You also own everything outright — no dependency on a third-party platform.

This is where Coastal Digital operates. Our Jacksonville projects typically run between $2,000 and $4,000 for most small business sites — giving you a fully custom build at a price that makes sense for a local business that's serious about growth.

Best for: Jacksonville small businesses that want a site that works as a real business asset without paying large-agency prices.

Tier 4: Large Agency or Enterprise ($15,000–$100,000+)

Enterprise-level projects — large e-commerce stores, complex web applications, multi-location businesses — fall here. These projects involve dedicated project managers, larger teams, extensive QA, and often custom backend development. For most Jacksonville small businesses, this tier is overkill.

Best for: Established businesses with complex requirements, large product catalogs, or custom application needs.

What Actually Drives the Price Up

Beyond the base build, several factors push the cost of any website higher. Understanding these helps you have a smarter conversation with whoever you hire.

  • E-commerce functionality — Adding a product catalog, cart, and checkout adds significant complexity. A Shopify or WooCommerce store built properly typically adds $2,000–$8,000 to the base cost.
  • Content creation — If you don't have copy, photos, or a logo, those cost extra. Professional copywriting for a 5-page site runs $500–$2,000. Photography varies widely.
  • SEO setup — A site built without SEO in mind is a wasted investment. Proper keyword research, page structure, local schema, and meta optimization should be included in any serious project. If an agency quotes you a price and SEO isn't mentioned, ask about it directly.
  • Ongoing maintenance — Websites need updates, security patches, and occasional edits. Monthly maintenance plans typically run $100–$500/month depending on scope.
  • Custom features — Booking systems, client portals, membership areas, and custom integrations all add cost. These are billed per feature, not as a flat rate.

The honest truth

A cheap website built without SEO, performance, or conversion in mind isn't saving you money — it's costing you customers. The question isn't just "how much does a website cost?" It's "how much is it costing me not to have a good one?"

How to Choose the Right Option for Your Business

Here's a simple framework. If you're a brand new business with no revenue yet — start with a simple Squarespace or build something minimal yourself and upgrade in 6-12 months. If you have an established business and your current site is embarrassing, slow, or invisible on Google — you need a proper custom build. The ROI on a well-built website for a local service business is usually very fast.

The most important thing is to be honest about what you need the site to do. If it needs to generate leads or bookings, build it right the first time. If it just needs to confirm you exist for people who already know your name, a simpler option is fine.

Questions to Ask Any Web Designer Before You Hire Them

  1. Do you build from scratch or use themes/templates?
  2. Is SEO included, or is that a separate cost?
  3. How will the site perform on mobile?
  4. What's the load time target?
  5. Who owns the site and code after launch?
  6. What's included in post-launch support?

Any designer worth hiring should have clear, confident answers to all six. Vague answers on ownership or SEO are red flags.
