How Long Does It Take to Build a Website?

The timeline varies, but for most Jacksonville small business sites, you're looking at 2 to 4 weeks from kickoff to launch. Here's what that actually looks like — and what causes projects to run longer.

One of the most common questions we get from Jacksonville business owners is how long a website build actually takes. The honest answer: it depends on scope — but for a clean, custom-built 5-page business site, 2 to 4 weeks is a realistic target from the day work begins to launch day.

What varies the timeline isn't usually the design or development — it's the steps that happen before and after, and how quickly the client can provide input and feedback. Here's a realistic breakdown of what each phase looks like.

The Typical Website Build Timeline

Week 1: Discovery and Design

The first week is about understanding your business before writing a single line of code. This means learning who your customers are, what makes you different from competitors, what you want visitors to do when they land on your site, and what you like (and don't like) visually.

From there, we move into the initial design — layout, color, typography, and the overall structure of the site. This is usually presented as a mockup or wireframe for your review before development starts.

What can slow this down: Delayed feedback on the initial design direction, or not having a clear idea of what pages you need. The clearer you are at the start, the faster everything moves.

Week 2–3: Development

Once the design direction is approved, development begins. This is where the site gets built — every page coded, every button tested, every form wired up. For a standard business site, this typically takes one to two weeks depending on the number of pages and any custom features.

During development, we're also handling the technical side: on-page SEO setup, page speed optimization, mobile testing across multiple screen sizes, and making sure everything is properly structured for Google to crawl.

What can slow this down: Scope changes mid-build — adding new pages or features after development has started adds time. The more locked-in the scope is before development begins, the smoother this phase goes.

Week 3–4: Content, Review, and Launch

The final phase involves plugging in all your real content — copy, photos, logos, and any other assets — and doing a full review pass. We test everything on desktop and mobile, check all links and forms, verify page speed, and make any final adjustments based on your feedback.

Once everything is approved, we handle the technical launch: pointing your domain, setting up SSL, submitting to Google Search Console, and confirming the live site is indexed and performing correctly.

What can slow this down: Content that isn't ready. This is the single biggest timeline killer. If photos need to be taken, copy needs to be written from scratch, or a logo doesn't exist yet — that adds time before anything can launch.

What Causes Projects to Run Longer

In our experience, the builds that go smoothest are the ones where the client comes in prepared. The ones that stretch beyond 4 weeks usually fall into one of these categories:

  • Content isn't ready — No copy, placeholder photos, or a logo that still needs to be designed. Have your content as ready as possible before kickoff.
  • Slow feedback cycles — If review rounds take a week each, a 3-week project becomes a 6-week project. Fast, decisive feedback keeps things moving.
  • Scope creep — "Can we add a blog?" or "Actually, we need an e-commerce section" mid-build. Scope changes are fine, but they move the timeline.
  • Multiple decision-makers — When five people need to approve every design change, things slow down. Designate one main point of contact.

What About Bigger or More Complex Sites?

The 2–4 week timeline applies to a standard small business website — typically 5 to 8 pages with a contact form and basic local SEO. More complex projects have longer timelines:

  • E-commerce stores — 4–8 weeks depending on catalog size and payment setup
  • Booking or scheduling systems — 4–6 weeks depending on integration complexity
  • Custom web applications — Quoted separately based on scope
How to get the fastest timeline

Come to kickoff with your logo, your brand colors, 3–5 websites you like the look of, a rough idea of what pages you need, and your key written content ready to go. That combination alone can cut weeks off a typical timeline.

When Should You Start?

The best time to start a website build is before you urgently need it. Rushing a website launch adds stress, invites mistakes, and often means cutting corners on the things that actually matter — performance, SEO, and mobile experience.

If you have a business opening, a product launch, or a campaign coming up — build the website 6 to 8 weeks out from the date you need it live. That gives you buffer for revisions and any unexpected delays without compromising quality.
